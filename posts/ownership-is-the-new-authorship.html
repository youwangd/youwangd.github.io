Ownership Is the New Authorship

The #1 thread on r/ExperiencedDevs this week is titled "Today I announced that I won't be reviewing AI generated PRs at company meeting." It hit 949 upvotes and 284 comments. The interesting number is in the comments: the top reply has 1,190 upvotes — more than the post itself. By the time most of us read it, the community had already rewritten the headline in real time. The rewrite is the only thing worth saving from the thread.

The thread that wasn't really about AI

The OP — an engineering manager at a "small AI startup" — took a stand at his all-hands. He was tired of reviewing PRs where the author couldn't answer basic questions about their own code. So he announced a policy: no more AI-generated PRs in review.

The top comment, by user vooglie, with that 1,190-upvote landslide:

"No one should be raising PRs they don't understand — whether it was written by AI or not."

The OP himself replied 66 minutes later, with 66 upvotes too — a corrective symmetry the universe could not resist:

"Yes sorry, I realised my title was misleading. The problem is not with AI but with the fact that the people who use it do not understand the code it generates."

That's the whole thread, compressed. A few hundred comments later, the community converged on a clean reframe from Future_Manager3217:

"The cleaner line is not 'I won't review AI-generated PRs'. It's 'I won't review PRs whose author can't own the diff'."

Same instinct, sharper knife. The new code-review contract isn't about who or what typed the characters. It's about whether the person whose name sits at the top of the PR can defend it.

Why "I won't review AI PRs" is the wrong knife

Three reasons the original framing breaks down on contact.

1. It's unenforceable. You can't reliably tell whether a diff was AI-generated. Style detectors are fooled by competent prompting. Even raw model output can be polished by a thoughtful dev in five minutes. The week you announce this policy is the week your team stops mentioning AI in commit messages — the problem doesn't go away, it just goes underground.

2. It punishes the wrong axis. A senior engineer can spend two hours reading docs and produce a diff she can defend line-by-line. A junior can spend two minutes prompting Claude and produce one he can also defend line-by-line — because he prompted as an amplifier, then read the output, then ran it through the obvious failure cases. Both PRs are fine. Conversely, both can be terrible: hand-written code can be ritualistic copy-paste from Stack Overflow that the author never thought through. Authorship and ownership are independent variables. Filtering on the first one ignores the dimension that actually matters.

3. The real failure mode is tool-agnostic. Reddit user Specialist_Golf8133 nailed it:

"Syntactically correct but structurally wrong is exactly the dangerous one. It passes CI and a casual read, and the happy path won't catch it either… the generator has no mental model of the system's contracts, so it can't know what it's violating."

That's not an AI bug. That's a pattern-matcher bug, and humans pattern-match too. The reason it feels like an AI bug now is that AI scaled it. Where one engineer used to produce one structurally-wrong PR per quarter and trip the on-call once, now ten engineers produce ten per week, and the structurally-wrong rate just outran review capacity. Another commenter (either-15-or-40) named the side-effect: "With AI code, you don't get any consistency between PRs. So you always have to check for everything which is exhausting." The headline blames the tool. The data is in the throughput.

The ownership packet

Future_Manager3217 didn't stop at the reframe. He proposed an actual artifact — a small "review packet" that has to land with every PR before it enters the queue. Lightly extended, this is the most concrete thing the thread produced:

  • Intent. One paragraph, in the author's own words, on why this change exists. Not a generated summary. Not a JIRA paste. The author writing.
  • Risk surface. Which contracts, invariants, or downstream consumers this could break. The "what I'd lose sleep over" section.
  • What I ran. Tests, types, lint — actual command lines and their output. "Tests passed locally" no longer counts as evidence.
  • What I personally verified. The screenshots, the curl outputs, the log excerpts where you actually watched the new code do the new thing.
  • What I still don't understand. An explicit "here be dragons" list. This is the line that distinguishes a competent AI-assisted dev from a vibe-coder. The vibe-coder leaves it blank because nothing scares him. The honest dev writes "I don't know why this race condition resolves on retry; I added tests around it and they pass, but I'd want a second eye on the lock semantics." That's a defensible packet.

The companion test, from jasie3k later in the thread:

"Code reviews nowadays have to be done during the call, you as a person raising the PR need to explain it in full… if you can't explain why something is being done without saying 'lol Claude did it this way' then it is a rejection."

Whatever the packet, that's its verbal shape. If you can't walk a reviewer through the diff on a five-minute screen-share without invoking your model's name as an explanation, the diff isn't ready. Notice — this works exactly the same way for a hand-written PR. The contract is direction-neutral. That's the point.

What survives in tooling, what dies

I write a CLI for agentic coding (SageCLI). Half of what I built looks different through this lens. Some of it ages well. Some of it doesn't.

What survives — and was always the right answer:

  • PLAN.md before code. SageCLI's plan-first contract is the intent paragraph. If the agent can't write a coherent PLAN.md, the human shouldn't be able to ship the PR. We added it to keep agents honest. Same artifact now keeps humans honest at submit time.
  • The external trail — msg send, inbox, scratch-log. We built these to keep agents legible to other agents. It turns out they're the audit log a reviewer needs to interview a vibe-coder. The reviewer reads the trail and asks the human author the questions the agent already answered. Disagreement is the signal.
  • --then validation chains. The "what I ran" section, auto-populated. We built it because models lie about test results. The same primitive shuts down the human auto-pilot of "yeah, looks good." Capture the command output, attach it to the packet, move on.

What dies:

  • "AI-generated PR" as a meaningful category. It isn't one. The new categories are defensible and undefensible. Anyone shipping a code-review tool whose primary axis is "detect AI" is solving the wrong problem. The tool that wins surfaces which PRs the author cannot answer questions about — and that's a tool that works on diffs, not on tokens.
  • The 10-tab review queue. Last week's piece called this out from the throughput angle; this week's thread is the deeper version. CI green is necessary, never sufficient. A diff that passes CI and that no human can defend is unreviewable, regardless of how it was produced. Stop optimizing for queue depth. Start optimizing for defensibility.

Read the correction

The thread had 284 comments and a 1,190-upvote correction sitting on top of a 949-upvote post. Read the correction, not the headline. The new code-review contract isn't "did a human write this?" — it's "can a human own this?"

Build your tooling around that question. Everything else is litigation.
